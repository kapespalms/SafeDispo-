The safety gate & level-of-care knowledge base
Every disposition passes through these topics. Reference only — does not replace clinical judgment or state-specific law. Tap a row to expand.
Tier 1 · The safety gate
1A · Suicide risk assessment

Purpose: identify patients at risk to set level of care and required safety interventions before discharge.

C-SSRS Screener — severity 0–5 + behaviors1,5

  • 6 core questions: wished dead · thoughts of killing self · thought how · some intent to act · plan + intent · ever done/started/prepared anything (→ if yes, within 3 months?)
  • LOW: Yes to Q1/Q2 only; Q3–Q6 all No
  • MODERATE: Yes to Q3 (how) OR behavior (Q6) >3 months ago
  • HIGH: Yes to Q4 (intent) and/or Q5 (plan + intent), OR behavior within 3 months
  • Positive screen → +LR >10 for suicide death at 3 mo; in ED, +LR >10 for attempt within 30 days. 2024 VA/DoD: may be the most useful universal-screening tool

PHQ-9 Item 9 — "better off dead / hurting yourself"

  • Any response other than "Not at all" predicts suicide & attempt
  • ~5% of primary-care depression patients report SI most/nearly every day → ~1% self-harm/attempt over 90 days8
  • Item 9 alone (vs full PHQ-9) not separately validated

ASQ — 4-item peds + adult ED screen4

  • Wished dead · family better off if dead · thoughts of killing self · ever tried
  • Any Yes → positive → full assessment; if all No → "thoughts right now?" (acuity). Joint Commission-recommended

Stanley-Brown Safety Planning — 6 steps before discharge11

  • (1) warning signs · (2) internal coping · (3) people/settings for distraction · (4) people to ask for help · (5) professionals/agencies · (6) make environment safe (lethal means)
  • Reduces suicidal behaviors 45–76% vs usual care; meta-analysis of 14 studies → fewer attempts + better follow-up
  • Superior to no-suicide contracts (no evidence; not recommended)

Lethal means counseling (Step 6)

  • Assess firearms, medication stockpiles, sharps, other means
  • Firearms → off-site storage with trusted person, gun lock, safe
  • Medications → limit supply, trusted person holds. Recommended by VA/DoD, AAP, Joint Commission
Disposition logic
  • LOW → community + safety plan + crisis resources + follow-up within 7 days
  • MODERATE → consider PHP/IOP; follow-up 48–72 h; safety plan + lethal means required
  • HIGH → inpatient (voluntary/involuntary), or crisis stabilization if no bed
1B · Violence / homicide risk

Purpose: identify acute risk to others; determine duty-to-warn and disposition. No single tool reliably predicts ED violence.

Structured tools (forensic/inpatient)

  • HCR-20 — 20-item historical/clinical/risk-management
  • STAMP — Staring, Tone, Anxiety, Mumbling, Pacing
  • Brøset — confusion, irritability, boisterousness, verbal threats, physical threats, attacks on objects

Key risk factors

  • History of violence (strongest predictor)
  • Active psychosis with command hallucinations / persecutory delusions
  • Intoxication (stimulants, PCP, alcohol); weapon access; specific identified victim; treatment non-adherence

Tarasoff / duty to warn12

  • Obligation (varies by state) to warn identifiable victims of a credible threat
  • Document: specific threat, identified victim, actions taken (victim + law-enforcement notification)
Disposition logic
  • No acute risk → standard
  • Elevated, no specific threat → enhanced safety planning, consider higher LOC, document
  • Specific threat + identifiable victim → duty to warn/protect, consider involuntary hold, notify law enforcement per state law
1C · Agitation management

De-escalation (first-line, always before meds)13

  • Calm voice, short sentences; offer choices (food/drink/seating)
  • Address unmet needs; clear respectful limits; safe distance; remove audience/stimulation; low-stimulation room

Pharmacologic — IM (cannot take PO)15,16

  • Droperidol 5–10 mg — most rapid; recent first-line
  • Olanzapine 10 mg — do NOT combine with IM benzo
  • Midazolam 5 mg — fastest sedation at 15 min; preferred for stimulant intox / alcohol withdrawal
  • Haloperidol 5–10 mg ± diphenhydramine 50 mg — slower onset

Rescue / extreme

  • Ketamine 4–5 mg/kg IM when other agents fail; continuous monitoring (pulse ox, ETCO2) after

Oral (cooperative)

  • Olanzapine 5–10 mg ODT; or risperidone 2 mg + lorazepam 2 mg PO

Monitoring & safety

  • Continuous pulse ox (no supplemental O2), VS q15 min until responsive, ETCO2 when available; watch respiratory depression, hypotension, EPS/dystonia, oversedation
  • Do NOT combine IM olanzapine + IM benzo; avoid routine antipsychotic+benzo combo
  • Benzos → alcohol/benzo withdrawal & stimulant intox; antipsychotics → known psychosis / undifferentiated agitation
1D · Medical clearance (medical screening exam)

AAEP minimum for ALL psychiatric patients17,18

  • Prefer "medical screening exam" over "medical clearance"
  • Thorough history (substances, meds, medical) · physical exam · vital signs incl. temperature · mental status exam

Labs — not routine if alert/cooperative, normal vitals, noncontributory history, normal exam19

  • Fingerstick glucose (AMS/diabetes) · urine pregnancy (childbearing age) · BAL (suspected intox)
  • UDS (rarely changes acute mgmt) · CMP (metabolic/renal) · CBC (infection) · TSH (new-onset)
  • CK — agitation/stimulants/restraint (most common cause of medical intervention in one study) · drug levels (Li/VPA/CBZ)

Screening tools21

  • SMART Checklist — sensitivity 83.3%, NPV 99.1% · TAPS sens 66.6% · Shah's spec 76.9% (all need validation)

Red flags → full workup first

  • Abnormal vitals · altered consciousness/delirium · new-onset psych symptoms age >40 without history · focal deficits · head trauma · significant comorbidities · first-episode psychosis
Disposition logic: medical condition → stabilize on medical service first; normal/cooperative → proceed to psych; borderline → document reasoning.
1E · Capacity evaluation

Appelbaum 4 components12

  • (1) Understanding · (2) Appreciation (applies to them) · (3) Reasoning (weigh risks/benefits) · (4) Communication of a consistent choice

Principles

  • Decision- and time-specific; ≠ competency (a court determination); can fluctuate (reassess)
  • Disagreeing with advice ≠ lacking capacity; psychiatric illness ≠ automatic incapacity
  • Neurocognitive disorders are the most common cause of impairment

Process

  • Identify the concern (consent vs refusal vs AMA vs dispositional)
  • Psychiatric interview + cognitive assessment; patient describes treatment/risks/benefits/alternatives/consequences in own words; assess each component; consider MacCAT-T
  • Document the specific decision, diagnosis, each component, reversibility, recommendation
Disposition logic: has capacity → patient decides (incl. AMA); lacks capacity → surrogate, consider guardianship, involuntary hold if safety concern.
1F · Involuntary commitment

Criteria (vary by state) — typically require ALL

  • Mental illness (intoxication alone may not qualify) · imminent danger to self/others OR gravely disabled · less-restrictive alternatives insufficient

Hold types

  • Emergency hold (e.g., 72-h, physician-initiated) · judicial commitment (court-ordered, longer) · outpatient commitment / AOT

Document & rights

  • Specific behaviors/statements showing danger; diagnosis; why less-restrictive insufficient; signature + time
  • Patients retain rights (attorney, hearing, refuse some treatments); state law governs duration, who initiates, "gravely disabled," intoxication holds
Tier 2 · Level-of-care determination
2A · LOCUS (psychiatric level of care)

Six dimensions (each 1–5)22

  • Risk of harm · functional status · medical/addictive/psychiatric comorbidity · recovery environment (stress) · treatment & recovery history · engagement & recovery status

Six levels

  • 1 Recovery maintenance (<1 visit/mo) · 2 Low-intensity community (weekly/biweekly) · 3 High-intensity community (IOP/ACT/day tx)
  • 4 Medically monitored non-residential (PHP) · 5 Medically monitored residential (residential/crisis stabilization) · 6 Medically managed residential (inpatient)

Scoring (total 6–30, approx)

  • 6–11→L1 · 12–15→L2 · 16–19→L3 · 20–22→L4 · 23–25→L5 · 26–30→L6
  • Any single dimension = 5 may warrant L5/L6 regardless of total. Joint Commission-recognized
2B · ASAM criteria (substance use level of care)

Six dimensions (4th ed.)25

  • Acute intoxication/withdrawal · biomedical conditions · psychiatric & cognitive · substance-use risks · recovery environment · person-centered (SDOH, preferences, motivation)

Levels

  • 1 Outpatient · 1.5 OP therapy · 1.7 Medically managed OP · 2.1 IOP · 2.5 High-intensity OP · 2.7 Medically managed IOP
  • 3.1 / 3.5 / 3.7 / 3.7-BIO residential · 4 Medically managed inpatient (x.7 = withdrawal management incl. benzo)

Principles

  • Person-centered, shared decisions; all programs co-occurring-capable; chronic-care model
  • Should NOT justify administrative discharge. VA/DoD: placement-outcome trials lacking — "neither for nor against," useful as multidimensional assessment
2C · Inpatient admission criteria

General indications29

  • Active SI with plan/intent (esp. recent attempt); active HI with identified victim + plan
  • Acute psychosis unable to self-care/dangerous; acute mania with dangerous behavior; severe self-neglect / grave disability
  • Med stabilization not safe outpatient (e.g., clozapine init); failed step-down with deterioration
  • Severe alcohol/sedative withdrawal (CIWA-Ar ≥20, DTs/seizure history); eating disorder with medical instability (BMI <15); catatonia

Factors associated with admission from ED30

  • SI/attempt · psychosis · involuntary presentation · prior hospitalization · intoxication + psych symptoms · homelessness
2D · PHP & IOP

PHP — ≥20 h/wk (≈5–6 h/day × 5)32

  • Multidisciplinary; intensive group + individual + meds; home each evening
  • Step-down from inpatient (most common), step-up from outpatient, stabilized acute suicidality needing monitoring, impairment too severe for IOP
  • >90% report symptom reduction

IOP — 9–19 h/wk (≈3 h/day × 3–5)34

  • Primarily group; step-down from PHP/inpatient, step-up from outpatient, crisis stabilization, SUD IOP
  • Goal: crisis management/stabilization; cost-effective; equal/superior satisfaction in some studies
2E · Crisis stabilization units
  • Short-term (<24–72 h), 24-hour-staffed observation as inpatient alternative; voluntary or involuntary
  • Brief assessment, med initiation, safety planning, outpatient linkage; diverts admission, reduces ED boarding
  • Indications: acute crisis that may resolve with brief intervention · SI without imminent plan/intent (observe to clarify) · intoxication needing observation · diagnostic uncertainty (medical vs psych) · no inpatient bed (bridge)
2F · Residential treatment
  • 24-hour supervised care below hospital level
  • Psychiatric residential: chronic SMI with functional impairment, failed outpatient
  • SUD residential: ASAM 3.1–3.7 · Dual-diagnosis: co-occurring SUD + psychiatric
  • Duration weeks–months
Tier 3 · Diagnosis-specific disposition
3A · Acute psychosis / schizophrenia
  • First-episode psychosis → coordinated specialty care (e.g., NAVIGATE); early intervention improves outcomes35
  • Admit: unable to self-care, dangerous behavior, command hallucinations with intent, can't maintain safety
  • Discharge: improving, adherent, safe environment, follow-up arranged
  • Consider LAI before discharge for adherence risk; follow-up within 7 days; ACT, supported employment, psychosocial rehab, NAMI
3B · Mania / bipolar emergency
  • Admit: severe mania with dangerous behavior, psychotic features, can't self-care, no sleep for days
  • Discharge: resolving + med started + safe environment
  • Start mood stabilizer (Li, VPA) or atypical; screen for bipolar before antidepressant (can precipitate mania)
  • MDQ: ≥7 symptoms positive (sens 73%, spec 90% in psych outpatients)37
3C · MDD with suicidality

PHQ-9 bands8

  • 0–4 minimal · 5–9 mild · 10–14 moderate (MDD cutoff; sens/spec 88%) · 15–19 mod-severe · 20–27 severe
  • ≥10 suggests MDD; ≥15 suggests active treatment; always assess Item 9 further regardless of total
  • Disposition by suicide-risk level (Tier 1A), not PHQ-9 alone
  • Start antidepressant (SSRI first-line); follow-up within 7 days; consider 24–72 h callback
3D · Substance intoxication
  • Alcohol: observe until BAL declining + ambulating safely, protect airway
  • Stimulant: monitor cardiac, agitation (benzos preferred), hyperthermia
  • Opioid/overdose: naloxone, observe ≥2 h post-naloxone (longer for long-acting), offer MOUD
  • Discharge: clinically sober, stable VS, no SI when sober, safe plan; screen for SUD + offer referral
3E · Substance withdrawal (CIWA-Ar, COWS, benzo)

Alcohol — CIWA-Ar (10 items, 0–67)39

  • <10 mild → supportive, consider gabapentin/carbamazepine, thiamine 100 mg
  • 10–18 moderate → benzodiazepines (chlordiazepoxide/diazepam, or lorazepam if liver disease), symptom-triggered
  • ≥19 severe → inpatient; inpatient if CIWA-Ar ≥20, DTs/seizure history, complicating conditions
  • Benzos cut seizure risk 84% vs placebo for CIWA-Ar ≥10

Opioid — COWS (11 items, 0–47)41

  • 5–12 mild · 13–24 moderate · 25–36 mod-severe · >36 severe
  • Buprenorphine induction at COWS ≥10 (target 12–16); 2–8 mg SL, repeat 2–4 mg q4h; IDU/mod-severe OUD often tolerate initial 8 mg
  • Precipitated-withdrawal risk with full agonists (fentanyl/methadone); not life-threatening but drives relapse — offer MOUD before discharge

Benzodiazepine27

  • Can be life-threatening (seizures); high-dose → taper in inpatient/medically managed residential (ASAM 3.7/4); gradual over weeks–months, no abrupt stop
3F · MOUD (medication for opioid use disorder)
  • Buprenorphine: ED/inpatient/outpatient; X-waiver eliminated (2023); maintenance 8–24 mg/day SL; MOUD follow-up within 72 h40
  • Methadone: OUD only via licensed OTP (not outpatient Rx); many OTPs take walk-ins
  • Naltrexone: oral 50 mg/day or IM XR 380 mg/month; opioid-free 7–10 days first; also for AUD; no abuse potential
3G · Personality disorder in crisis (BPD)
  • Brief hospitalization for acute safety only — prolonged admission can be counterproductive
  • Safety planning (Stanley-Brown) is primary; avoid reinforcing ED visits as coping
  • Refer to DBT (strongest evidence); consider PHP/IOP with DBT; coordinate with outpatient provider before discharge
3H · Delirium
  • Delirium is a MEDICAL emergency — do NOT disposition to a psychiatric facility
  • Identify & treat the underlying cause (infection, metabolic, medication, withdrawal, hypoxia); manage on a medical service
